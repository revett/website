**I was introduced to reading plans by [Odysseas](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCQjBsscIa_mgEnSvWpm_9vw) on YouTube.** Reading plans are curated lists of books centred around a theme or goal. They're important because they provide structure and direction to your reading habit.

I use reading plans to explore neglected topics, challenge myself with new ideas, and engage in deliberate learning of a specific area. This post **records the reading plans I'm currently pursuing.**

Instead of targeting a specific number of books yearly, I focus on completing reading plans. This approach equates to the same goal through intentional topic exploration, avoiding the "make number go up" *BookTok* mentality.

## Format

**Each reading plan consists of 8 books.** Why 8? It's enough to dive deep into a topic without being overwhelming. It's a number that feels achievable yet substantial. Each book in the plan is carefully selected to provide **a balanced and comprehensive view of the theme.**

## Lists

### Classics

> *Currently reading, started July 2024.*

1. **"The Picture of Dorian Gray" by Oscar Wilde** — A philosophical novel about beauty, hedonism, and the corruption of the soul.
2. **"Don Quixote" by Miguel de Cervantes** — A humorous and profound exploration of reality and idealism.
3. **"Pride and Prejudice" by Jane Austen** — A witty examination of love, marriage, and social class in Georgian England.
4. **"Crime and Punishment" by Fyodor Dostoevsky** — A psychological thriller exploring guilt, morality, and redemption.
5. **"The Odyssey" by Homer** — An ancient Greek epic following Odysseus's journey home after the Trojan War.
6. **"Wuthering Heights" by Emily Brontë** — A passionate and haunting tale of love and revenge on the Yorkshire moors.
7. **"The Count of Monte Cristo" by Alexandre Dumas** — An intricate story of betrayal, imprisonment, and elaborate revenge.
8. **"Frankenstein" by Mary Shelley** — A Gothic horror exploring the boundaries of science and the nature of humanity.

### Modern Classics

1. **"Siddhartha" by Hermann Hesse** — A spiritual journey of self-discovery set in ancient India.
2. **"The Grapes of Wrath" by John Steinbeck** — A portrayal of the Great Depression and the American migrant experience.
3. **"To Kill a Mockingbird" by Harper Lee** — An exploration of racial injustice and loss of innocence in the American South.
4. **"1984" by George Orwell** — A vision of a totalitarian future that remains chillingly relevant.
5. **"One Hundred Years of Solitude" by Gabriel García Márquez** — A multi-generational saga blending reality and magic.
6. **"The Catcher in the Rye" by J.D. Salinger** — A candid portrayal of teenage alienation and angst.
7. **"Beloved" by Toni Morrison** — A haunting tale of slavery and its aftermath in post-Civil War America.
8. **"The Great Gatsby" by F. Scott Fitzgerald** — A critique of the American Dream set in the Roaring Twenties.
